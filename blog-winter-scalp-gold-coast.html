Hair & Scalp Tips

Why your scalp itches in August

By Takashi Iwata Owner & Head Spa Practitioner 5 min read

Every year, from about mid-July, the same three complaints walk through my door. It itches. It flakes. It has gone oily and I have not changed anything. People assume they have developed a condition. Almost always, they have just had a Queensland winter.

What a Gold Coast winter actually does to a scalp

We do not have real cold here, so nobody expects winter scalp. But three things change in August whether you notice them or not.

Your showers got hotter. This is the big one, and nobody ever admits it. On a 30-degree morning you rinse in tepid water. On a 12-degree morning you stand under water hot enough to redden your skin, and you stand under it longer. Hot water strips the scalp’s protective barrier faster than any shampoo does.

The air dried out. Winter air on the Coast carries far less moisture, and if you are running a heater, air-conditioning on reverse cycle, or sitting in a car with the vents on your head, that number drops again.

You stopped rinsing the salt and chlorine off as often. In summer you are in the water and out of it and under a shower three times a day. In winter, product sits on the scalp for longer between proper washes.

The loop that makes it worse

Here is the trap, and I watch people walk into it every single winter.

The scalp dries out and starts to flake. The flaking looks like dandruff. So you buy a stronger, more stripping anti-dandruff shampoo — which removes even more of the barrier that was already gone — so the scalp dries out further and flakes more, so you scrub harder.

Nine times out of ten, the flaking scalp I see in August is dehydrated, not dirty. It is being punished for a crime it did not commit. Takashi

The oily version of this is the same loop wearing a different coat. Strip a scalp aggressively enough and it compensates by producing more oil, faster — so your roots are greasy by lunchtime while your ends are still dry as straw. You wash more. It gets worse.

Four things you can do this week, for free

  • Turn the shower down. Not cold — just not hot. If your skin is pink when you get out, the water was hot enough to be doing damage. This single change fixes more winter scalps than any product I could sell you.
  • Wash with fingertips, never nails. You are cleansing skin, not scrubbing a pan. Small circular movements. Scratching an itchy scalp feels wonderful and makes it worse every time.
  • Rinse for twice as long as you think you need to. Most people leave conditioner and product residue sitting at the roots, and residue is tomorrow’s build-up.
  • Dry your scalp, not just your hair. Going to bed with a damp scalp in winter is an invitation for irritation. Get the roots properly dry — this is also, incidentally, why a wet head spa that sends you home with wet hair is doing half a job.

When it is worth having someone actually look

If the itch, the flaking or the oiliness has run for more than a month and nothing you have changed at home has shifted it, stop guessing from the hair and have the scalp itself read.

That is where every session here starts: a consultation and a trichology-informed scalp diagnosis, before any product touches your head. Dehydrated and oily can look identical from a metre away and need opposite treatment — which is exactly why buying a bottle based on how your hair feels is a coin toss.

Then the treatment is chosen for what is actually there. Cleanse away the build-up properly; put the moisture back; work the tension out of a scalp that has spent the winter tight. The whole ninety minutes is described here, if you have never had one.

August is, genuinely, the best month of the year to do this. Fix it now and your scalp goes into summer in condition, rather than spending until Christmas recovering. If any of the three above sounded like your own head, mention it when you book and I will make time for it.
