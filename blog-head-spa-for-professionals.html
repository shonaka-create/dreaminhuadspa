Head Spa Guide

For people who think for a living

By Takashi Iwata Owner & Head Spa Practitioner 4 min read

A surprising number of the people in my chair are doctors, lawyers, accountants and business owners. That was not a marketing decision. It is simply who keeps coming back, and after a few years I think I understand why.

The tension in your head is not a metaphor

Put your fingertips on the top of your head, right now, and try to move the skin. Not slide your fingers across it — actually shift the scalp itself, the way you can shift the skin on the back of your hand.

For most people who work at a screen and make decisions all day, it barely moves. That is not tightness you are imagining. The muscle and fascia over the skull are genuinely gripped, blood flow to the follicle drops, and hair starts growing in weaker and lying flatter. You can usually feel a hard ridge along the base of the skull where the neck meets it.

This is the physical residue of concentration. Hours of holding a jaw slightly clenched, eyes fixed, shoulders lifted a centimetre without noticing. It accumulates, and it does not release on its own — not over a weekend, and not over a two-week holiday, as many of my guests have discovered.

Why a massage does not fix it, but this does

A remedial massage will work on your back and shoulders and stop at the hairline. That is the convention, and it leaves the tightest part untouched.

The Japanese head spa starts where the massage stops. Layered with the traditions of Indian head massage, the work goes into the scalp, the base of the skull, the temples and the neck — the exact places that seize up when you spend your day thinking hard. And because it is a wet treatment, warm water is running through it for most of the session, which does something to the nervous system that no amount of dry pressure achieves.

The guests who arrive most certain they will not be able to switch off are the ones who go under fastest. Twenty years, and it has never once failed to be true. Takashi

The part I think actually matters

Here is my real theory, for what it is worth.

If you are a surgeon, a partner, a principal — you spend your entire working day being the person who decides. Every question in the building arrives at you. Even your leisure is decision-shaped: which restaurant, which flight, which school.

For ninety minutes in this room, nothing is asked of you. You do not choose the products; I choose them, based on what I find on your scalp that day. You do not manage the time; there is no express version to opt into and no clock being watched. You do not even have to talk — say so at the start and I will work in silence.

It is not the massage that resets people. It is the total absence of anything requiring a judgement. That is a rarer commodity than it sounds, and it is the one thing money genuinely cannot buy anywhere else on your calendar.

The practical objections, answered

“I cannot turn up to a meeting looking like I have been to a salon.” You will not. Every treatment ends with a full professional blow-dry, included — you leave styled and presentable, not damp and obvious. This is exactly why the finish is not optional here.

“Ninety minutes is a lot of time.” It is. It is also less than the time you will lose to the fog of a bad week, and it is the shortest treatment I am willing to offer, because the ones under an hour do not work on a head like yours.

“Can I get a cut at the same time?” Yes, and most of the professionals who come here do exactly that. Head Spa & Cut, $260 — one appointment, 120 to 135 minutes, and you have handled both the reset and the haircut you have been postponing for a month. Booked separately those two services would be $360.

“I am worried about thinning and I do not want to discuss it.” Then do not. But know that the scalp diagnosis at the start will give you an honest, unembarrassed read on what is actually happening up there — which is more than most people in your position have ever had, and rather more useful than a search engine at midnight.

We are open Tuesday to Saturday, and Saturdays go first. If your week does not have ninety spare minutes in it, that is not an argument against booking. It is the argument for it.
