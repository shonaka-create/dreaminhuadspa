Head Spa Guide

Wet, dry, or just a scalp massage?

By Takashi Iwata Owner & Head Spa Practitioner 5 min read

Three treatments share the words “head” and “spa” on Gold Coast menus, and they do genuinely different things. If you book the wrong one for what you actually want, you will walk out thinking head spas are overrated. They are not. You just bought the wrong one.

Here is the honest comparison, from someone who has performed all three.

The 15-minute scalp massage

What it is: a pair of hands on your head while your colour develops, or a short add-on at the basin. Usually $30–$50, often thrown in free.

What it does: it feels lovely and it relieves some surface tension. That is all it is designed to do, and it does it.

What it does not do: it does not clean the scalp, treat the scalp, or change anything about it by tomorrow. Nothing is diagnosed, nothing is rinsed, nothing is fed. It is a nice fifteen minutes, not an intervention.

Book it if: you are already in the chair and it is free. Do not book it expecting your scalp to be different afterwards.

The dry head spa

What it is: a longer, structured scalp and head massage performed without water, usually seated, often 30–60 minutes. Roughly $60–$100.

What it does: proper relaxation. Good dry head spa work reaches the muscle and fascia over the skull, releases genuine tension, and can leave you floating. Some people cry. It is a real treatment and I have nothing bad to say about it.

What it does not do: it cannot cleanse. Sebum, product residue and build-up sit around the follicle whether you massage them or not — and with no water and no rinse, they are all still there when you stand up. It also cannot deliver anything into the scalp: you cannot rinse out what you did not put in, so nothing much gets put in.

Book it if: you want relaxation and nothing else, and you want to keep your hair dry and your budget low. That is a completely legitimate thing to want.

The wet head spa — the Japanese version

What it is: the full treatment, performed at a basin with warm water running through the hair for a large part of the session. Ninety minutes or more. At Dreamin’, $220 with the blow-dry included.

What it does: two things a dry treatment structurally cannot.

  • It actually removes what is on your scalp. Layered cleansing lifts the build-up that ordinary shampooing leaves sitting at the root — the stuff that makes roots turn greasy within a day while the ends stay dry.
  • It puts something back. Once the scalp is genuinely clean, it can absorb. Nutrients and moisture go in, and get worked in through massage rather than smeared on top.

Cleanse, then feed. That sequence is the entire logic of the Japanese head spa, and it is impossible without water. Everything else — the pressure, the rhythm, the traditions of Indian head massage that we layer in — is built on top of it.

A dry head spa treats how your head feels. A wet head spa treats the skin your hair grows out of. Both are worth doing. Only one of them changes what grows next. Takashi

The comparison, in one table

  • Want to feel wonderful for an hour and keep your hair dry? Dry head spa.
  • Want your scalp cleaned, treated and fed — and your hair to behave differently for the next month? Wet head spa.
  • Want fifteen free minutes while your colour develops? Take the scalp massage, obviously.

The one thing to check before you book anything

Ask whether the blow-dry is included. It sounds trivial. It is not.

A wet head spa ends with your hair soaked. If the treatment does not include a professional finish, you are going home to do it yourself — which for most people means the good work of ninety minutes ends in a rushed twenty with a bad hairdryer. A blow-dry is a $110 service in its own right, so a wet head spa without one is not the bargain it appears to be.

Every head spa here includes it. Not as a courtesy — as the last step of the treatment. You do not leave this room with wet hair.

If you have never had the wet version, here is exactly what happens in the ninety minutes. And if your scalp has been misbehaving lately, it is probably trying to tell you something specific.
