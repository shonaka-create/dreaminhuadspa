Gifts

A gift for the man who buys his own things

By Takashi Iwata Owner & Head Spa Practitioner 4 min read

Father’s Day in Australia falls on Sunday 6 September 2026. Which gives you about six weeks to solve the same problem you had last year: he does not want anything, and he will tell you so.

I hear about this from the other side of the chair. Every year in late August and early September, my room fills with men who did not book themselves in. Their daughter did. Their wife did. Their son, who could not face buying another bottle of whisky, did.

And almost every one of them says the same thing on the way out: I would never have booked this myself. That is not a complaint. That is the entire point of the gift.

Why the “he has everything” man is actually easy to buy for

The man who buys his own things has already solved every problem that money solves. Another gadget is not a gift to him; it is an errand he now has to find a drawer for.

What he has not bought himself — what men in particular almost never buy themselves — is permission to do nothing for ninety minutes. Not a massage he has to book and justify. Not a spa day with a robe and a fruit platter, which most men find faintly embarrassing. Ninety minutes in a private room where somebody who has done this for twenty years works on his head, and he is not required to be useful to anyone.

Most of my guests fall asleep. The ones who arrive insisting they never fall asleep anywhere are, without fail, the ones who go under first. Takashi

What he actually gets

It is worth being specific, because “head spa” sounds vague and he will want to know what he is walking into.

  • A private room. One guest at a time. No open salon floor, no small talk with strangers, no waiting area.
  • A flat shampoo stand, imported from Japan. His neck is never bent back over a basin rim, which is the difference between a treatment he endures and one he sleeps through. The story behind that stand is here.
  • A proper diagnosis. If he has been quietly worried about thinning, itching or an oily scalp — and a lot of men are, and almost none of them say so — he will get a straight answer from someone looking at the actual skin.
  • A blow-dry, included. He leaves looking sharp, not damp. For most men this is the moment the whole thing clicks.

Three ways to give it, depending on your dad

If he will happily accept a spa treatment: an e-Gift Voucher for Head Spa & Dry ($220). Ninety minutes, blow-dry included, nothing for him to organise but the date.

If he needs a practical excuse to accept it: Head Spa & Cut ($260). He was going to need a haircut anyway — and this way the head spa arrives as a bonus attached to something he considers legitimate. Booked separately those two services would be $360, so it is also the better buy by a hundred dollars.

If he is the type who would rather choose: a voucher for any amount. It is redeemable across the whole menu, including a straightforward men’s cut at $85 if that is genuinely all he wants.

One last thing, from twenty years of watching this happen. The men who are hardest to buy for are almost always the men who have spent decades doing things for other people, and have simply lost the habit of being on the receiving end. Ninety minutes is a very small thing to give back. It just happens to be the one thing he was never going to buy himself.
