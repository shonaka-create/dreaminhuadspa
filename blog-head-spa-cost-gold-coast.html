Head Spa Guide

How much should a head spa actually cost?

By Takashi Iwata Owner & Head Spa Practitioner 6 min read

Search for a head spa on the Gold Coast and you will find prices from about $60 to $260. That is a four-fold spread for something with the same name, and nobody explains it. So let me — including where my own $220 goes.

I own a head spa in Southport. That makes me an interested party, and you should read this knowing it. What I can offer you in return is the thing the price comparison sites cannot: I know exactly what it costs to deliver each version of this treatment, because I have delivered all of them.

Why the same word covers four different products

“Head spa” is not a protected term. It gets used for at least four things, and they have almost nothing in common except the word.

  • The 15-minute add-on (around $30–$50). A scalp massage tacked onto a colour service while the tint develops. Pleasant. It is a massage, not a treatment.
  • The dry head spa (around $60–$100). Performed without water, usually in a chair. It genuinely relaxes you. It does not clean or feed the scalp, because nothing is ever rinsed. If relaxation is all you want, this is honest value.
  • The express wet head spa (around $110–$150 for 40–60 minutes). Water is involved, so the scalp does get cleansed. The compromise is time — and, almost always, the finish: you leave with wet hair and blow-dry it yourself in the car park.
  • The full wet head spa (around $200–$260 for 90 minutes or more). Diagnosis, cleanse, treatment, massage, and a professional blow-dry to finish. This is the Japanese version, and it is what I do.

None of these is a rip-off. They are different products at appropriate prices. The mistake is comparing the sticker on one with the sticker on another, as if they were the same thing in different sizes.

The arithmetic nobody shows you

Here is my own menu, and I am going to argue against myself with it for a moment.

A Wash & Blow Dry at Dreamin’ is $110. That is a real, standalone, sixty-to-ninety minute service that people book on its own, and $110 is roughly what a professional blow-dry costs anywhere on the Coast.

My Head Spa & Dry is $220, and it includes that blow-dry.

Take the $110 blow-dry out of the $220, and the ninety-minute head spa itself is the other $110. Under $2.50 a minute — for the diagnosis, the cleanse, the treatment and the massage. Takashi

Now run the same sum on a 50-minute express head spa at, say, $130. That works out at $2.60 a minute — and the blow-dry is not in it. Add one, and you are either paying again or doing it yourself with a hotel-grade hairdryer and no mirror behind you.

I am not telling you the cheaper treatment is bad. I am telling you it is not cheaper in the way the number suggests. Per minute of actual work on your scalp, the gap between a $130 express and a $220 full service is far narrower than the price tags imply — and one of them ends with you looking finished.

What you are paying for, line by line

Time on the clock. The single biggest cost in this business is the room and the practitioner. A ninety-minute treatment costs a salon roughly twice what a forty-five-minute one costs, before anyone has opened a bottle. Every head spa priced under about $150 is, by simple arithmetic, a shorter head spa. There is no third option where the time is free.

How many guests are in the room. I take one guest at a time. That is a deliberate and expensive choice — a salon floor running three chairs earns three times what I do in the same ninety minutes. You are, quite literally, paying for the empty chairs.

Whether anyone looked at your scalp first. A consultation and trichology-informed scalp diagnosis takes ten to fifteen minutes and produces a treatment chosen for your head rather than a routine applied to it. Cheap head spas skip this, because it is unbillable time.

Whether the finish is included. In Japan, the blow-dry is part of the treatment, not a line item you get upsold at the end. I brought that rule with me and I will not bend on it. If a menu quotes you for a head spa and the blow-dry is extra, the quoted price is not the price.

So when is the expensive one not worth it?

Genuinely: if all you want is to feel relaxed for an hour, book the dry head spa and save your money. It works. I would not be insulted.

The full wet head spa earns its price when there is something to actually treat — an itchy or flaking scalp, roots that turn oily within a day, hair that has gone flat — or when you need to walk out of the appointment and straight into the rest of your life looking like you did not come from a hairdresser.

And it earns it when you have been disappointed before. Most of the guests who book my signature Head Spa & Cut at $260 are not first-timers to the category. They are people who tried a cheap one, found it underwhelming, and came looking for the version that works.

One more number, because it is the best one on the menu

Head Spa & Dry is $220. Cut & Style is $140. Booked separately, that is $360 and two appointments.

Booked together as Head Spa & Cut, it is $260 — a hundred dollars less, in a single unbroken visit, with the cut done on a scalp that has just been properly read. It is the most-booked service here for a reason, and it is the only place on this menu where I would tell you the maths is obviously in your favour.

Whatever you decide, decide it on the minutes and the finish, not on the headline. That is the whole of my advice, and it applies just as well when you are choosing someone else.
