Scalp & Hair Health

Understanding an oily scalp

By Takashi Iwata Owner & Head Spa Practitioner 6 min read

An oily scalp can be frustrating.

Many people believe that washing their hair more often is the solution, but in many cases, it can actually make the problem worse.

At Dreamin’, we believe that understanding why your scalp becomes oily is the first step toward achieving healthier hair and a healthier scalp.

Let's explore what causes an oily scalp, how to identify it, and what you can do to restore balance.

What Is an Oily Scalp?

Your scalp naturally produces an oil called sebum.

Sebum plays an essential role in keeping your scalp healthy. It helps:

  • Protect the scalp
  • Prevent moisture loss
  • Keep hair soft and flexible
  • Support the scalp's natural protective barrier

Problems begin when your sebaceous glands produce more sebum than your scalp actually needs.

Excess oil builds up on the scalp, causing hair to look greasy, flat, and heavy—even shortly after washing.

Signs You May Have an Oily Scalp

Common signs include:

  • Hair becomes oily within 24 hours after shampooing
  • Hair feels heavy or lacks volume
  • A greasy feeling on the scalp
  • Frequent itching
  • Product or oil build-up around the hair follicles
  • An unpleasant scalp odor
  • Oily dandruff or flakes

An oily scalp may also create an environment where buildup collects around the follicles, making it more difficult to maintain optimal scalp health.

What Causes an Oily Scalp?

Several factors can increase sebum production.

Genetics

Some people naturally produce more oil than others.

Hormonal Changes

Hormones directly influence your sebaceous glands, which is why teenagers, pregnant women, and people experiencing hormonal changes often notice an oilier scalp.

Overwashing

Washing too frequently can strip away your scalp's natural oils.

In response, your scalp may produce even more oil in an attempt to restore balance.

Using the Wrong Hair Care Products

Heavy conditioners, silicone buildup, or products that aren't suited to your scalp type can contribute to excess oil and buildup.

Stress

Stress can affect hormone levels, which may increase oil production for some individuals.

Lifestyle

Sleep, nutrition, hydration, and overall health all contribute to maintaining a balanced scalp.

What Happens If an Oily Scalp Is Left Untreated?

Although an oily scalp isn't necessarily unhealthy, excessive oil buildup can contribute to:

  • Persistent itching
  • Scalp irritation
  • Unpleasant odor
  • Product buildup
  • Reduced hair volume
  • A scalp environment that is more difficult to keep clean

Maintaining a balanced scalp environment is important for healthy-looking hair.

How to Care for an Oily Scalp

Choose a Gentle Scalp-Focused Shampoo

The goal isn't to remove every drop of oil.

The goal is to restore your scalp's natural balance without over-drying it.

Wash Your Scalp Properly

Focus on cleansing your scalp—not just your hair.

Use your fingertips (never your nails) to gently massage your scalp for several minutes before rinsing thoroughly.

Apply Conditioner Correctly

Conditioner should generally be applied to the mid-lengths and ends of your hair unless the product is specifically designed for scalp application.

Avoid Scratching Your Scalp

Scratching may irritate the scalp and stimulate additional oil production.

Maintain a Healthy Lifestyle

Managing stress, getting quality sleep, staying hydrated, and eating a balanced diet all support healthier skin—including your scalp.
