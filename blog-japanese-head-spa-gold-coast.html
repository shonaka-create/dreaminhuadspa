Owner's Story

Why I brought the Japanese head spa to the Gold Coast

By Takashi Iwata Owner & Head Spa Practitioner 4 min read

I spent twenty years learning to cut hair. It took me most of that time to understand that the hair was never really the point — the scalp was.

I trained in the hair industry in Japan and have spent more than two decades behind the chair: cutting, colouring, styling. I competed at brand level with Goldwell, KMS and L'Oréal Professionnel, and I have worked backstage at major fashion parades in Sydney and Melbourne, where a hundred heads have to be finished perfectly and finished fast.

That work teaches you speed and precision. What it does not teach you is patience — and patience, it turns out, is the entire craft of the head spa.

The thing I couldn't find here

When I settled on the Gold Coast, I looked for a proper head spa the way you look for a good coffee in a new city — expecting to find one within a week. I didn't.

There were scalp massages, offered as a fifteen-minute extra while the colour developed. There were dry head spas, done without water, which feel wonderful and treat nothing. What I could not find was the thing I grew up with in Japan: a full wet head spa, where warm water runs through the hair for the better part of an hour, where the scalp is genuinely cleansed and then genuinely fed, and where nobody is looking at the clock.

So I built it.

The shampoo stand

The first thing I imported was a completely flat shampoo stand from Japan. It sounds like a small detail. It is not.

On a standard salon basin, your neck is bent back over a rim for the entire treatment. You can tolerate that for a five-minute wash. You cannot relax into it for ninety minutes — your body simply will not let go. The flat stand takes the strain out of the neck entirely, and that is the difference between a treatment you endure and a treatment you fall asleep in.

Most of my guests do fall asleep. I take it as the review that matters.

Reading the scalp before touching it

Every session here begins with a consultation and a scalp diagnosis, informed by trichology — the study of hair and scalp health. I want to see the condition of your scalp before I decide what to put on it.

An oily scalp and a dry, flaking one can look similar from the outside and need almost opposite treatment. A scalp that is tight and undernourished will not grow strong hair no matter how good the shampoo is. So the products and the technique are chosen for the head in front of me, on the day — never from a fixed routine.

Then we layer the methods: what the Japanese beauty industry has refined over decades, together with the traditions of Indian head massage. Lift away what shouldn't be there. Put back what should.

Why the blow-dry is never an extra

One rule I brought with me and will not bend on: you do not leave here with wet hair.

In Japan, the finish is part of the treatment — it is not a line item you are upsold at the end. Every head spa at Dreamin' includes a full blow-dry, because the work isn't done until you look in the mirror and see it. You walk out styled, and ready for wherever you are going next.

Twenty years of technique, and it still comes down to something simple: take the time, treat the scalp, finish the job properly. Takashi

That is the whole idea behind Dreamin'. One guest at a time, a private space, ninety minutes minimum, and a head that feels like it has been given back to you.

If you have never had a wet head spa — a real one — I would genuinely like to be the one to show you what it is.
